## 1. What We Are and What We Do

### The One-Liner
> **"Stop quoting for free. Start getting paid before you estimate."**

### The Expanded Version (for sales conversations and landing pages)
> "We install a front-end system that captures every enquiry, qualifies the serious ones, educates homeowners on your process, and moves them into a paid pre-construction step — before you pick up the phone."

### What PreBuild Autopilot Actually Is
PreBuild Autopilot is a **done-for-you automated preconstruction sales system** built inside GoHighLevel, delivered as a custom installation for individual residential builders. We build it for them, configure it to their process, and hand it over running.

It automates the journey from first enquiry to paid preliminary agreement — a process that currently happens manually (or not at all) for most custom builders in Australia.

The builder's first human interaction with a new prospect becomes a **paid, pre-qualified, pre-educated** conversation. Not a free site visit with someone who was never serious.

### What We Sell (v1 Deliverables)
1. **Branded Lead Intake Form** — captures qualifying data from website, Facebook, or referrals
2. **Smart Qualification Survey** — scored questions with auto-routing (Hot / Warm / Cold)
3. **Automated Education Sequence** — multi-touch email/SMS explaining the builder's pre-construction process
4. **Digital Proposal + E-Signature** — preliminary building agreement with legally valid e-sign
5. **Stripe Payment Collection** — preliminary fee collected on signature; money goes directly to the builder
6. **Client Stage Tracker Portal** — branded portal showing the client's current project stage
7. **Builder Dashboard** — all leads, scores, pipeline stages, and payments visible at a glance
8. **Follow-Up Automation** — reminders for stalled leads, re-engagement sequences

### What We Do NOT Sell in v1
- AI voice agent or chatbot
- Estimating, takeoff, or quantity surveying
- Build-phase project management
- Document version control or variation management
- Buildxact API sync
- Lead generation (ads, SEO, content marketing)

## 2. The Problem We Solve

### The Core Problem in One Sentence
Custom builders are haemorrhaging time and money on the gap between first enquiry and paid pre-construction commitment, with no systematic way to filter serious prospects from tire-kickers before investing hours of free work.

### The Problem in Builder Language
> *"You're spending your nights quoting jobs that go nowhere. Nine out of ten people who ask for a quote ghost you or were never serious. We turn your enquiries into paid preliminary agreements — before you spend a single hour estimating."*

### Why This Problem Exists
The construction industry evolved backend-first. Builders have excellent tools for estimating (Buildxact), job management, and compliance — but almost nothing for the front-end sales process between "someone contacted us" and "they paid a preliminary fee."

The dominant culture is the **free quote** — a legacy expectation set by volume builders who cross-subsidise quoting costs with scale. Custom builders (5–30 homes/year) cannot afford this model but have no systematic alternative. They either:
- Give away detailed quotes and absorb the loss
- Try to charge for preliminary work but can't sell it because homeowners haven't been educated on why it matters
- Lose leads to slower follow-up because they're on site all day

### The Value Chain of Loss
```
Lead Arrives → Free quoting / no qualification → Budget mismatch discovered too late
     ↓                                                         ↓
No speed-to-lead             →           Serious leads go to whoever picks up first
     ↓                                                         ↓
No education automation      →           Builder spends 3–5 hrs explaining process manually
     ↓                                                         ↓
Weak prelim fee conversion   →           $500–$6K in preliminary revenue left on the table
     ↓                                                         ↓
Manual admin / fragile process →         Leads cool off, fall through the cracks
```

### The Financial Reality
- Builders spend 40–80 hours/month on free quotes with a 10–20% win rate
- At even $100/hour internal cost, that's $4,000–$8,000/month in unbilled time per year
- 3,217 builder insolvencies in Australia in 2024 — poor upfront scoping and underquoted fixed-price work are key drivers
- Builders who collect paid prelims and stage deposits are structurally more resilient — they have cash in hand before committing resources

## 3. The Two Audiences

PreBuild Autopilot has two distinct audiences. Content must be written for the correct one.

### Audience A: The Builder (Our Direct Customer)
This is who we sell to. The builder is the person paying for and installing the system. They are the decision-maker, the primary pain-holder, and the one we need to convince.

**→ All cold email, ads, landing pages, sales scripts, and outbound content is written FOR THE BUILDER.**

### Audience B: The Homeowner (The Builder's Customer)
This is who uses the system after we sell it to the builder. The homeowner goes through the automated qualification survey, receives the education sequence, and signs the preliminary agreement. We do not directly market to homeowners — but we must deeply understand their psychology because the system we build must speak to them compellingly.

**→ Education sequence emails, portal content, qualification surveys, and proposal documents are written FOR THE HOMEOWNER (on behalf of the builder).**

The distinction matters enormously for content writing. A landing page headline that resonates with a builder ("Stop quoting for free") would confuse a homeowner. An education email that works for a homeowner ("Here's how our 3-stage process protects your investment") would bore a builder.

## 4. ICP: The Builder — Deep Profile

### Demographic and Business Profile

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Role** | Owner-operator / principal builder |
| **Business size** | 1–5 staff (often just themselves + a bookkeeper or admin) |
| **Annual volume** | 5–30 custom homes or major renovations per year |
| **Revenue range** | $1M–$15M annually |
| **Geography** | NSW, VIC, QLD (primary); SA, WA (secondary) |
| **Enquiry volume** | 5–30 per month |
| **Average job value** | $300K–$2M+ construction contract |
| **Current tools** | Buildxact ($149–$599/mo), Xero, basic website, Facebook page |
| **Budget signal** | Already paying for software; not price-resistant if ROI is clear |

### Psychographic Profile — How They Think

The typical ICP builder is:
- **Technically skilled and proud of their craft** — they build excellent homes and they know it
- **Stretched thin** — wearing too many hats (builder, estimator, sales, admin, project manager)
- **Commercially frustrated** — they know free quoting is broken but don't have a clear alternative
- **Skeptical of software salespeople** — they've been burned by overpromised tools before
- **Action-oriented** — they respond to tangible outcomes and specific numbers, not vague promises
- **Time-constrained** — they are literally on a building site from 6am and dealing with trades, clients, and councils simultaneously
- **Reluctant marketer** — they want their quality work to speak for itself; systematic lead management feels foreign

### Their Day-to-Day Reality
A typical morning for our ICP builder:
- 5:30am: Check phone. 2 new enquiry emails from last night. Both need replies.
- 6:00am: On site. Can't reply to enquiries.
- 8:30am: Gap between tasks. Quickly replies to one enquiry. Forgets the other.
- 2:00pm: Client calls about quote from last week that hasn't arrived. The builder had to deprioritise it because they were managing a variation on another job.
- 5:30pm: Off site. Sits down to write the quote they promised 3 days ago. 2 hours later — it might go nowhere.
- 7:30pm: Enquiry from 5:30pm arrives in inbox. Too late to reply tonight.

**This is not laziness. This is a structural problem: the front-end of their business is running on willpower and memory instead of systems.**

### What They Want (Often Unsaid)
1. To only talk to people who are serious and can afford their work
2. To stop spending evenings on quotes that ghost them
3. To look more professional without spending hours redesigning their process
4. To be on site building, not at home explaining what a preliminary agreement is
5. To get paid for the pre-construction work they already do (but currently give away for free)
6. To grow without hiring another person just to handle admin

### What They Fear
1. **Wasting time on the wrong leads** — a site meeting that costs 3 hours and produces nothing
2. **Being ghosted after investing serious estimation effort** — the most emotionally charged pain
3. **Looking unprofessional** — their work is excellent but their systems make them look like a one-person operation
4. **Missing a serious lead** — knowing a good job slipped through because they were too busy to follow up
5. **Paying for another tool that doesn't get used** — they've invested in software before and not fully adopted it

### Qualifying Signals (They Are a Good Fit If...)
- Website has a generic "Contact Us" or "Get a Free Quote" form
- They describe their work as custom, bespoke, or architecturally designed
- They are the primary estimator as well as the builder (double pain)
- They have tried to charge for preliminary work at some point
- They are running Google or Facebook ads (investing in leads but likely leaking them)
- They express frustration with time-wasters or tire-kickers in any conversation
- Their Instagram/Facebook shows consistent quality project photography (active business, takes pride in presentation)

### Disqualifying Signals (Not a Fit If...)
- Volume builder producing 50+ homes per year (different problem, different solution)
- Commercial-only builder (different sales cycle entirely)
- Gets fewer than 3–5 enquiries per month (lead generation is the problem, not conversion)
- Already has a dedicated sales/admin person handling all front-end (less pain)
- Refuses outright to consider charging for pre-construction work (fundamental mismatch)

## 5. Builder Archetypes

There are five builder archetypes within our ICP. The system fits some better than others. All content and email personalisation should be calibrated to the archetype where possible.

### Archetype 1: Custom / Architectural Builder ★★★★★ (Primary)
**Profile:** Builds high-specification, architecturally designed custom homes. Typically 3–12 builds per year. Job values $500K–$2M+. Clients come with architect's plans or concept drawings.

**What they feel acutely:**
- The 120+ hours required for a detailed fixed-price quote on a complex custom home
- Budget shock when a client's $800K expectation meets a $1.3M reality — after all the quoting work is done
- Losing large jobs to competitors who picked up faster or presented more professionally

**Best message hook:** The cost of free detailed quoting. "You're doing $10K of free estimating work for someone who might already be getting 3 quotes."

**Prelim fee structure:** Two-tier (Budget Estimate first, then Detailed Quote)

---

### Archetype 2: Renovation / Extension Specialist ★★★★☆ (Primary)
**Profile:** Kitchen, bathroom, second-storey additions, major renovations. Typically 5–20 projects per year. Job values $80K–$800K.

**What they feel acutely:**
- Scope uncertainty — clients enquire with vague descriptions ("I want to extend the back of the house") and the builder can't price until they've done a site inspection
- High volume of "just want a ballpark" enquiries from people who are nowhere near ready
- Missed calls — they are on someone else's property all day, not at a desk

**Best message hook:** "When a lead calls and you're on site, what happens to that call?" + the voicemail stat.

**Prelim fee structure:** Single-tier or two-tier depending on project complexity

---

### Archetype 3: Design-Build Firm ★★★★★ (Primary)
**Profile:** Integrated design and construction firm. May have in-house or partner architects. Handles the full journey from concept to contract. Typically 5–15 projects per year at premium price points.

**What they feel acutely:**
- The gap between "client comes to us with an idea" and "client signs a contract" can be 3–12 months of unpaid relationship-building
- Clients who use their design input to get a concept, then take it elsewhere for a cheaper build
- The difficulty of communicating the value of their integrated process to clients comparing them against split architecture + build quotes

**Best message hook:** Education burden — "Do your clients understand what they're paying for and why your process protects their investment before your first call?"

**Prelim fee structure:** Two-tier (Design Development + Construction Pricing)

---

### Archetype 4: Small Builder / Solo Operator ★★★☆☆ (Secondary)
**Profile:** One or two-person operation. 3–8 builds per year. Mix of custom homes and renovation work. Runs everything — sales, site management, estimating, admin.

**What they feel acutely:**
- Overwhelm — too many roles, not enough hours
- No admin to handle follow-up or scheduling
- Every hour spent on a quote that doesn't convert is a personal loss

**Best message hook:** Time — "You're doing everything. How many hours a month are you spending on quotes that go nowhere?"

**Prelim fee structure:** Often no fee (free quotes model), or single-tier if they've graduated to charging

---

### Archetype 5: Builder Who Already Charges for Prelims ★★★★★ (Highest Priority for Sales)
**Profile:** Spans archetypes 1–4 but has already adopted the paid preliminary model. Knows the concept. Has a process (often manual and based on Word docs + email). Wants to convert more leads and spend less time on the admin.

**What they feel acutely:**
- Their prelim conversion rate is lower than it should be — homeowners still push back on the fee
- The process is manual, inconsistent, and dependent on the builder remembering to do things
- They look less professional than their quality deserves

**Best message hook:** Conversion — "You've got the right model. We automate the delivery so you convert more and spend less time on the paperwork."

**Why they're highest priority:** They already understand the value of preliminary fees. Zero education required on the concept. The sell is "we make your existing model faster and more consistent." Shortest path to yes.

## 6. The Homeowner — Secondary Audience

We write content for homeowners on behalf of builders. This is the education sequence, portal content, and proposal documentation.

### Homeowner Profile
- Typically a couple (35–55 years old) planning their biggest financial decision
- Have been researching builders for weeks or months before making contact
- Anxious about budget blowouts (they've heard the horror stories)
- Uncertain about the process — don't know what's normal, what to expect, or what they're entitled to ask
- Influenced by whoever they speak to first and whoever makes them feel most understood

### What Homeowners Fear
- Paying more than they budgeted — and not knowing until it's too late to change course
- Signing a contract with a builder who won't communicate during the build
- Paying for a preliminary consultation and feeling like they got nothing for it ("money for nothing")
- Being locked in before they fully understand what they're getting
- The builder they like being out of reach or unresponsive

### What Homeowners Want
- Transparency — what is this process, what does it cost, what do I get?
- Confidence that the builder is competent and professional
- A clear next step at every stage — no ambiguity about what happens now
- To feel like a valued stakeholder, not just a data point
- Protection — assurance that their deposit or preliminary fee is safe and purposeful

### Content Principles for Writing to Homeowners (on behalf of builders)
- Warm, clear, and reassuring — never cold or transactional
- Explain the "why" behind every step — homeowners who understand the process accept it
- Position the preliminary fee as protection for them, not a cost for the builder
- Use the builder's voice — the content should feel personal, not generic
- Acknowledge the emotional weight of the decision — this is the biggest thing they'll ever pay for

### Key Reframes for the Preliminary Fee Conversation
| How homeowners see it | How to reframe it |
|---|---|
| "I'm paying before anything is agreed" | "You're protecting yourself from budget shock by knowing the real number before signing a contract" |
| "Other builders quote for free" | "Volume builders cross-subsidise quoting with scale. A custom builder invests 100+ hours in your quote. This fee covers that commitment." |
| "I don't know what I'm paying for" | "You'll receive [specific deliverables] — here's a sample of exactly what the report looks like" |
| "What if I don't like the price?" | "The fee is refunded when you proceed to a building contract with us. It's an investment in certainty, not a sunk cost." |

## 7. Pain Points: The Full Map

### For the Builder (Primary Audience)

#### P1: Free Quoting Waste — THE PRIMARY PAIN
- **What it is:** Builders spend 40–80 hours per month producing detailed quotes for leads who ghost, price-shop, or were never serious
- **Why it matters:** At even $100/hour, that's $4,000–$8,000/month in unbillable time. Plus the emotional cost of evenings and weekends spent on work that produces nothing.
- **Evidence strength:** STRONG — confirmed across HIA, industry bodies, Reddit builder communities, and direct builder interviews
- **In their own words:** "I spend my nights quoting jobs that go nowhere." / "Nine out of ten people who ask for a quote ghost you."
- **How to use in content:** Primary cold email hook, landing page headline, pain-led sales scripts

#### P2: No Lead Qualification System
- **What it is:** Every enquiry gets treated equally. Budget misalignment, land not settled, timeline 2 years away — the builder finds out after the site meeting.
- **Why it matters:** 2–5 hours wasted per unqualified lead. 10 enquiries/month = potentially 20–50 hours on leads that should never have reached the builder.
- **How to use in content:** Secondary hook for cold email; primary angle for demo conversations

#### P3: Missed Calls and Slow Follow-Up
- **What it is:** Builders are on site from 6am. They cannot answer phones or reply to emails in real time. Leads sit unanswered for hours or days.
- **Evidence:** Research shows contractors miss ~35% of incoming calls; 80% of callers who get voicemail don't leave a message — they call the next builder.
- **Who it hits hardest:** Builders spending money on Google or Facebook ads (they're paying for leads they're then losing)
- **How to use in content:** Strong hook for Segment E (ad-running builders). Email follow-up angle. "You're paying for leads and losing a third to voicemail."

#### P4: Can't Sell the Preliminary Fee
- **What it is:** Builders who try to charge for pre-construction work face homeowner resistance. The fee feels like "money for nothing" because the builder hasn't demonstrated value before asking.
- **Root cause:** No automated education before the fee conversation. The homeowner's first encounter with the fee concept is the invoice.
- **How to use in content:** Mid-sequence email angle (email A3). Demo close objection handler. Strongest in sales conversations after they've acknowledged the free quoting problem.

#### P5: Education Burden
- **What it is:** Builders spend 3–5 hours per qualified prospect explaining their process, what preliminary work costs, what the homeowner gets, and why paying for it protects them.
- **Why it matters:** 10 prospects/month × 3 hours = 30 hours/month on education that should happen automatically before the first call.
- **How to use in content:** Thread B (proof-led emails). Demo walkthrough moment. Best in content targeting Archetype 3 (design-build, where process complexity is high).

#### P6: Admin Chaos and Process Inconsistency
- **What it is:** The current preliminary agreement process is 5+ manual touchpoints — email, Word doc, phone call, invoice, follow-up. Each touchpoint is a potential failure. Admin forgets. Clients delay. Payment and agreement are disconnected.
- **How to use in content:** Demo context (show the manual process vs. the automated version side by side). Less effective as a cold email hook — too granular for a first touch.

#### P7: Lead Leakage
- **What it is:** Qualified leads who expressed interest fall through because the builder's follow-up is inconsistent. The lead goes cold because nothing happened for 10 days.
- **How to use in content:** Secondary angle supporting missed calls / slow follow-up. "The best lead you've had this month might already be talking to someone else."

### For the Homeowner (Secondary Audience)

#### H1: Budget Uncertainty
The homeowner's #1 fear is paying more than they expected. The preliminary process exists to solve this — but most homeowners don't know that until they've been educated.

#### H2: Process Opacity
Most homeowners have no idea what the pre-construction journey looks like, what each stage involves, or what's normal. They feel uncertain and anxious.

#### H3: "Money for Nothing" Perception of Prelim Fees
Homeowners see the preliminary fee as a cost to them with no clear benefit. The reframe is: it's protection — it tells you the real number before you sign the building contract.

#### H4: Fear of Being Locked In
Signing anything feels like commitment. The system should acknowledge this and provide reassurance — clear terms, clear deliverables, refund policy where applicable.

#### H5: Responsiveness Anxiety
Homeowners frequently report frustration with builders who are hard to reach. The education sequence and portal must make them feel heard and informed, not ignored.

## 11. Voice, Tone, and Style Guide

### Overall Tone
**Direct. Peer-level. Commercially grounded. No fluff.**

We speak to builders like a knowledgeable industry peer — not like a software salesperson. We are not trying to impress them with technical language or marketing polish. We are naming a problem they live with every day and showing them a specific solution.

### Tone Breakdown by Channel

| Channel | Tone | Length | Style |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cold email | Conversational, direct, curious | Short (100–150 words) | One pain point, one CTA |
| Follow-up email | Even shorter, same thread | Very short (50–80 words) | Add one piece of value, re-ask |
| Sales call script | Listening-led, diagnostic | N/A | Ask first, frame second |
| Landing page | Clear, outcome-led, confident | Scannable | Headers + short paragraphs |
| Demo script | Confident, show-don't-tell | 15 min | Homeowner journey first |
| Education sequence (homeowner) | Warm, reassuring, knowledgeable | Medium (200–350 words) | Process-explaining |
| Social content | Peer observation, not selling | Short | Insight + nod to the problem |

### Writing Principles
1. **Name the problem before you name the solution.** Builders need to feel understood before they'll listen to a pitch.
2. **Specific over vague.** "40 hours a month" beats "lots of time." "$5K of your time" beats "significant cost."
3. **One idea per paragraph.** Dense text doesn't get read on a phone screen between site tasks.
4. **End with one clear action.** Every piece of content should point to exactly one next step.
5. **Never sound like a marketer.** If it sounds like copy, rewrite it as a conversation.
6. **Use questions as hooks.** Questions get builders thinking about their own experience before you tell them anything.

## 16. Cold Email Writing Rules

Every cold email must follow these rules:

### Structure
1. **Subject line** — curiosity or pattern-interrupt; 5–8 words; no exclamation points
2. **Opening line** — a question, observation, or pain statement; never start with "I" or "My name is"
3. **The hook** — 1–2 sentences naming a specific problem
4. **The frame** — 1 sentence describing what we've built and who it's for
5. **The CTA** — one ask: 15-minute walkthrough
6. **Sign-off** — first name only (Jerald); no company logo, job title, or marketing footer

### Hard Rules
- Never exceed 150 words
- Never include product feature lists
- Never include pricing
- Never include screenshots or attachments on a first email
- Never use "I hope this email finds you well" or any variant
- Never open with "My name is" or "I work for"
- One CTA per email — never give more than one option
- Always write in plain text style — no headers, no bullets in the email body
- Personalisation must feel human — AI-generated personalisation that is factually wrong is worse than no personalisation

### Thread Logic
- Email A1 and B1 are sent simultaneously (different days: A1 Day 1, B1 Day 2)
- Follow-up emails reference the prior email in the same thread ("following up on the below")
- Email A4 is the permission close — gives an easy out while surfacing latent interest
- After 4–5 emails with no reply: sequence complete; do not send more

### Personalisation Principles
When personalising cold emails using builder research:
- Reference their specific type of work (custom homes, extensions, design-build) — not a generic "builder"
- Reference their geography if it adds specificity ("builders in [suburb/region]")
- Reference any ads they're running if confirmed via Meta Ad Library
- Do not fabricate specifics (project names, client names, awards) that cannot be verified
- A genuine, specific observation about their business (from their website or social media) is worth more than any compliment

## 17. Landing Page Writing Rules

### Structure (In Order)
1. **Headline** — outcome-led, specific, uses their language. Not clever. Clear.
2. **Sub-headline** — one sentence expanding the headline promise
3. **Pain statement** — 2–3 sentences confirming the problem (make them nod)
4. **The fix** — brief, plain-language description of what the system does
5. **What's included** — short list, outcome-oriented (not feature-oriented)
6. **Proof element** — testimonial or case example (even a short one)
7. **CTA** — single button: "Book a 15-Minute Demo"
8. **Guarantee** — short statement reducing risk

### Rules
- Must load fast on mobile (builders check email on their phones)
- No scroll required to reach the CTA on desktop
- One CTA only — no "contact us" form, no newsletter signup, no secondary links
- No pricing on the page — pricing is a sales conversation, not a landing page element
- No technical language, no GHL branding, no screenshots of backend dashboards
- The headline must pass the "5-second test" — a builder who lands on this page must immediately understand what it does and who it's for

- "How custom builders qualify leads, educate clients, and collect preliminary fees — automatically."

## 20. Market Context: Why Now

### The Industry Crisis That Creates the Opening

Australian residential construction is in structural stress:
- **3,217 builder insolvencies in 2024** — a record number, driven by fixed-price contracts signed on underquoted work
- **Poor upfront scoping** and free quote culture are contributing factors to insolvency
- **Industry bodies** (HIA, MBAWA, Master Builders) are actively advising builders to charge for preliminary work or face financial risk
- **Homeowner expectations** have been raised by digital experiences in other industries (banking, insurance, health) — builders whose process feels informal are losing to competitors who look more structured

The timing matters: the industry is under pressure to professionalise the front end of the sales process, and the advice to do so is coming from trusted industry bodies — not software salespeople. Our solution executes the change they're being told to make.

### Why Custom Builders Are the Right Market
- High per-job investment in free quoting (unlike trade services at $200–$500 per visit, custom builder quoting costs $2,000–$15,000 in unbilled hours)
- The preliminary fee model is culturally established — HIA and Master Builders both publish standard preliminary agreement templates
- The market is fragmented — no dominant player for the front-end sales process
- Existing tools (Buildxact, Wunderbuild) are backend-first; the front-end gap is real and unaddressed
