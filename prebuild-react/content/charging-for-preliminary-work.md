Most Australian custom builders give away the most valuable thing they own. Not materials. Not labour. Judgement.

A detailed quote on a custom home takes somewhere between 15 and 40 hours of skilled work. Site assessment, cost planning, subcontractor pricing, drawings cross-checked line by line. On a 20% win rate, four out of every five of those are done for nothing.

The maths is not subtle. Ten quotes a year at 20 hours each, at $150 an hour, with two of them signing, is 160 unpaid hours. That is a month of work you never billed, every single year.

This guide covers what the builders who solved it actually did.

## Why free quoting persists

It is not ignorance. Every builder who quotes for free knows what it costs. Three things keep it in place.

**The fear of losing the job.** If the builder down the road quotes free and you do not, the assumption is that the homeowner picks him. That fear is real but it is measuring the wrong thing. You are not competing for the quote. You are competing for the build.

**The awkwardness of asking.** Most builders can price a job to the dollar and still cannot say "that costs $2,500" without their voice changing. This is the actual barrier, and it is not a pricing problem. It is a process problem, which matters because processes can be fixed and personalities cannot.

**No structure to hang it on.** A fee with nothing around it is a demand. A fee attached to a named package with defined deliverables is a professional service. Same money, completely different conversation.

## What the successful models look like

Several Australian builders have made this work publicly. The patterns are consistent enough to copy.

**The staged preliminary agreement.** A South Australian custom builder runs a Preliminary Services Agreement at $3,000 to $5,000, credited back against the build. The homeowner receives a soil report, engineering recommendations, full planning drawings and a fixed price. The framing is that a free quote is the dangerous option, because a number produced without site investigation is a guess that gets corrected later, during construction, at their expense.

**The nominal filter.** A Sydney renovation specialist runs two stages. A free ballpark budget conversation, then a charge of $1,000 to $2,500 for detailed scope work. Their words: the charge is wiped if the client proceeds, and it is really only there to weed out the people who were never serious.

**The full feasibility.** A NSW builder charges a preliminary agreement fee of around $14,500, tied into a fixed price contract pathway, with a service fee payable if the client walks after engagement.

The range is wide. What does not vary is the structure underneath.

## The four things that have to be true

Across every model that worked, four conditions were met. Where builders have tried to charge and failed, at least one was missing.

### 1. Timing

The fee is never introduced on the first call. It comes after a free discovery conversation and usually after a site visit, once the builder has demonstrated they know something the homeowner does not.

Asking for money before you have shown value is a transaction. Asking afterwards is a next step.

### 2. Packaging

It cannot be called a quote fee. Charging for a quote sounds like charging for a sales document, and nobody pays for a sales document.

The models that work name the thing: Preliminary Services Agreement, Feasibility and Vision Package, Pre-Construction Due Diligence. Each is tied to deliverables the homeowner keeps whatever happens. The soil test is theirs. The contour survey is theirs. The engineering recommendations are theirs.

That is the difference between buying something and paying a toll.

### 3. Justification

The reason has to be about their risk, not your time.

"I spend too long on quotes that go nowhere" is true, and it is the wrong sentence. It makes your problem their problem.

The version that works sounds like this: we do not guess the price of your home, and to give you a fixed price we need to know what is under the ground and exactly what is going into the walls. This process is what makes your contract price accurate instead of an estimate.

Every word of that is about protecting them.

### 4. Risk reversal

Almost every working model credits the fee against the build. Some refund it outright. The homeowner needs to understand that proceeding costs them nothing extra.

Miss any one of these four and adoption fails. Builders who charge $400 with no package, no credit and no framing report that homeowners walk. That is not evidence that charging does not work. It is evidence that charging without structure does not work.

## What it does to your numbers

The obvious benefit is getting paid for preliminary work. It is not the main one.

The main one is what happens to the quotes you still do. When a homeowner pays before you price, they have made a decision. Sunk cost and consistency do the rest. Builders running structured paid preliminaries report conversion from agreement to build well above what any free quote pipeline produces, because the people who reach that stage have already chosen.

The second benefit is time. If you send ten quotes a year and four of those people were never going to build with anyone, a preliminary fee removes them before you open your estimating software.

The third is the one nobody expects. Serious clients respect it. A homeowner spending $900,000 on a house is reassured, not offended, by a builder with a professional process. The people who object are, reliably, the people who were shopping.

## If you already charge

Plenty of builders have a preliminary fee and still lose weeks to people who never sign. Charging is not the finish line.

Two leaks survive it.

**Enquiries that never reach the paid stage.** Every call, site visit and first consult with someone who does not proceed is still unpaid. The homeowner funds the estimate, not the courtship.

**Paid quotes that go quiet.** These are the expensive ones. The client paid you to produce a document, which means they were serious, and then nothing. Follow-up research consistently finds the sixth attempt is often the one that converts, and most builders stop at one or two.

If you charge already, your problem is not the fee. It is what happens either side of it.

## Where to start

Do not start with the fee. Start with the process it sits inside.

1. Write down what a homeowner gets for the money, as a list of deliverables they keep.
2. Give it a name that is not "quote fee".
3. Decide the credit structure and say it out loud in the first conversation.
4. Decide when in your sequence the fee is introduced, and never move it earlier.
5. Write the justification in their language, about their risk, and practise saying it until your voice does not change.

The fee is the easy part. The process is what makes it possible to charge.

## Common questions

### Will I lose jobs by charging for quotes?

You will lose enquiries. That is the point of a filter. What the builders running this report is that the jobs lost were disproportionately the ones that were never going to sign. The measure that matters is not enquiries received, it is hours spent per signed build.

### How much should I charge for a preliminary agreement?

Australian builders commonly run between $1,000 and $5,000 for residential custom work, scaling with the deliverables included. Higher tiers exist where full interior design and engineering are bundled. The number matters much less than whether it is credited back and what the homeowner keeps.

### What if the client says other builders quote for free?

They do, and that is worth acknowledging directly. The response is not to defend the fee, it is to explain the difference in what they receive. A free quote is an estimate produced without site investigation. A paid preliminary produces a fixed price backed by soil, survey and engineering. Those are different products.

### Do I refund the fee if they do not proceed?

Most working models do not refund it, they credit it against the build if the client proceeds. The homeowner keeps the deliverables either way, which is what makes it fair. Refunding outright removes the filter and puts you back where you started.

### Does this work for renovations and extensions, not just new builds?

Yes, usually at a lower number. Renovation specialists tend to run a smaller nominal charge, often $1,000 to $2,500, because the scope work is less extensive. The structure is identical.
