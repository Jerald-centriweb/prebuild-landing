The debate is usually framed as principle. Either you believe builders should be paid for their expertise, or you believe quoting is the cost of doing business.

Framed that way it is unresolvable, because both camps are describing real experience. It is more useful to compare what each model actually does to your week and your pipeline.

## The two models

| | Free quote | Paid preliminary |
|---|---|---|
| Who pays for the estimate | You | The homeowner |
| Typical enquiry volume | Higher | Lower |
| Time per signed job | High | Lower |
| Who it attracts | Everyone, including price checkers | People who have decided to build |
| Main leak | Unpaid estimating hours | Enquiries that never reach the paid step |
| Barrier to adopting | None | The conversation, and the process behind it |
| Typical conversion after the step | 10% to 25% of quotes | Materially higher, because payment is a decision |

Neither row is a knockout. They are different trades.

## What free quoting really costs

The cost is not the estimate itself. It is the estimates that go nowhere.

Ten detailed quotes a year at twenty hours each is two hundred hours. At a 20% win rate, one hundred and sixty of those hours were unpaid, which at $150 an hour is $24,000 of your own time. For most builders that is understated, because the twenty hours excludes the calls, the site visits and the revisions.

There is a second cost that is harder to see. Free quoting means you cannot afford to do the investigation properly. Nobody orders a soil test to produce a free estimate. So the number you give is a guess with a contingency in it, and either the contingency loses you the job or the guess causes trouble during construction.

## What paid preliminaries really cost

The obvious cost is enquiries. Some people will not pay, and a proportion of those would have built with you.

The less obvious cost is that it does not work without structure. A fee introduced on the first call, with no named package, no deliverables the client keeps and no credit against the build, gets declined. Builders who try that version conclude the market will not pay, when what actually happened is that they asked for money without offering anything.

The third cost is that it does not solve everything. Builders who charge still lose time on enquiries that never reach the paid step, and still lose paid quotes that go quiet afterwards. The fee moves the leak, it does not close it.

## Which clients each model attracts

This is the part that decides it for most builders.

A free quote is free for the homeowner to request, so the request costs them nothing and means nothing. You will receive requests from people benchmarking, people insurance-quoting, people who have already chosen a builder and need a comparison, and people who are genuinely serious. They arrive looking identical.

A paid preliminary costs something, so requesting it is a decision. That decision does two things: it removes the people who were never proceeding, and it commits the people who do. Payment before pricing is the difference between someone shopping and someone starting.

Where this cuts against you is early-stage clients who are genuinely serious but not ready to spend yet. A model with no free entry point loses them.

## The hybrid most builders end up running

Very few builders who charge run a single gate. The common shape is two steps.

**Step one, free.** A conversation and often a site visit. Ballpark range, not a quote. Costs you an hour or two, qualifies budget, timeline, project type and site, and lets you demonstrate you know something.

**Step two, paid.** The detailed work. Soil, survey, engineering, planning drawings, fixed price. Credited against the build if they proceed.

This keeps the front door open, so early-stage serious clients are not turned away, while nothing expensive happens before someone has committed.

The Sydney renovation model puts it plainly: the charge is wiped if the client proceeds, and it is really only there to weed out the people who were never serious.

## How to choose

Answer three questions honestly.

**Do you have enough enquiries?** If you are short of enquiries, a paid gate will make that worse before it makes it better. Fix volume first. A filter on a trickle produces nothing.

**Where is your time actually going?** If you send few quotes but each takes forty hours, your leak is estimating and a paid preliminary addresses it directly. If you send many quotes cheaply but most never reach a decision, your leak is qualification and follow-up, which a fee will not fix.

**Can you say the number out loud?** This sounds flippant and it is the real constraint. A builder who introduces a fee apologetically will have it negotiated away, and will conclude it does not work. If the process behind it is not solid enough for you to state it plainly, build the process first.

## The thing both models share

Whichever you run, the quotes that hurt are the ones where you found out too late.

The homeowner whose budget was never close. The job outside your profitable band. The client whose land had not settled. Free or paid, the estimate on that job was wasted, and it was wasted before anybody discussed price.

That is why the more useful question is not whether to charge. It is what you know before you start pricing.

## Common questions

### Should builders charge for quotes?

For a detailed, site-investigated fixed price, most builders eventually should, because it is skilled professional work with real third-party costs in it. For an initial ballpark conversation, usually not. The models that work in Australia charge for the second and give away the first.

### Will I lose work if I start charging?

You will lose enquiries. Whether you lose work depends on whether the enquiries you lose were converting. Builders who track it generally find hours per signed build fall sharply while signed builds hold.

### Can I run both at once?

Yes, and most builders who charge effectively do. A free first conversation followed by a paid detailed stage is the most common structure in Australian residential building.

### What if my competitors all quote for free?

They do, and that is an argument for differentiating rather than matching. Competing on who will do the most unpaid work is a race with no winner. The counter-position is that a free estimate produced without site investigation is a guess, and a paid preliminary produces a price that holds.
