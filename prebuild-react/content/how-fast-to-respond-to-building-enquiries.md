A homeowner sends an enquiry on Tuesday morning. You are on site, phone in the ute, and you see it at six. Wednesday runs away from you. Thursday you call.

Two days. That feels responsive by builder standards.

By then, in a lot of cases, the job is gone. Not lost on price, not lost on your work. Lost because somebody else answered first.

## What the timing actually does

The research on response time in high-value considered purchases is unusually consistent. Responding within five minutes makes you several times more likely to qualify the lead than responding within an hour. The odds fall off sharply after the first hour and keep falling.

The mechanism is not that fast builders are better builders. It is that a homeowner who has just sent an enquiry is, at that exact moment, thinking about their build. Their attention is on it. An hour later they are back at work. Two days later they have sent three more enquiries and spoken to somebody who called back.

You are not competing on quality at this point. You are competing on being present when they are paying attention.

## Why builders are slow, and why it is not laziness

Every builder knows they should ring back faster. The reasons they do not are structural.

**You are on site.** The work that pays you today is the work in front of you. Stopping to take an enquiry costs real progress.

**Enquiries arrive in the worst format.** A web form goes to an inbox you check at night. A missed call has no context. Neither tells you whether this is a serious client or somebody pricing a fence.

**You do not know which ones deserve interrupting for.** Since they all look identical at the point of arrival, the safe move is to deal with all of them later, which means dealing with all of them slowly.

That last one is the real problem, and it is worth noticing what it implies. Speed is a qualification problem wearing a time-management costume.

## What a fast response does not have to be

The common objection is that a five-minute response means dropping the trowel and having a sales conversation from a roof. It does not.

The first response has one job: acknowledge, and hold their attention. It does not have to price anything, it does not have to answer their questions, and it does not have to be you.

A first response that works looks roughly like this. It arrives within minutes. It confirms a person has it. It says what happens next and when. And it asks one or two things that start qualifying them.

That is a message, not a meeting. It can go out while you are still on the scaffold, and it buys you the rest of the day to reply properly.

## The three-layer version

Builders who have solved this usually run something like three layers.

**Immediate, automatic.** An acknowledgement that fires the moment an enquiry lands, day or night. Confirms receipt, sets the expectation, starts the qualification. Nobody has to do anything for this to happen.

**Same day, human but brief.** A call or a message from you or whoever handles the front end. Short. The purpose is to find out whether this is worth a site visit, not to sell.

**Structured, scheduled.** The site visit or discovery conversation, booked in properly, with the qualifying questions asked the same way every time.

Most builders have layer three and nothing else. Layer one is what changes the numbers, and it is the layer that costs the least effort because it happens without you.

## The out of hours problem

A substantial share of building enquiries arrive outside working hours. Evenings and Sundays are when homeowners sit down and think about their house.

If your response depends on somebody being awake, you are structurally slow for a large part of your enquiry flow. This is the single strongest argument for an automatic first response: not that it is faster during the day, but that it exists at all at 9pm on a Sunday.

## What to measure

If you want to know whether this is a problem for you, two numbers tell you.

**Time from enquiry to first contact.** Not your impression of it. Look at the last ten enquiries and check the timestamps. Most builders are surprised.

**How many enquiries never got a response at all.** This is the uncomfortable one. On a busy month, enquiries fall through. They do not announce themselves, because a homeowner who never hears back does not send a follow-up complaining. They just go elsewhere and you never learn they existed.

## Where it sits against everything else

Speed is not the most important thing in your front end. Qualification matters more, because a fast response to a lead that was never going to build is just a faster way to waste time.

But speed is the cheapest thing to fix. Qualification requires deciding what you want and asking better questions. Follow-up requires a sequence and discipline. A first response that fires in under five minutes requires setting it up once.

That is why it is usually the first thing to change.

## Common questions

### Does a five-minute response really matter that much?

For the first contact, yes. The effect is well documented across considered purchases: contacting a lead within five minutes makes qualification several times more likely than waiting an hour, and the curve keeps falling after that.

### Should the first response be automated?

The acknowledgement, yes. The conversation, no. Homeowners are not offended by an immediate message confirming their enquiry arrived and telling them what happens next. They are offended by an automated message pretending to be a person, and by never hearing anything at all.

### What if I respond fast and the lead turns out to be junk?

Then you found out fast, which is the point. A quick acknowledgement that also asks two qualifying questions costs you nothing and sorts the enquiry sooner.

### I get most enquiries at night. Should I reply then?

Not personally. Set up something that acknowledges immediately and tells them when a person will call. The enquiry stays warm and you keep your evening.
