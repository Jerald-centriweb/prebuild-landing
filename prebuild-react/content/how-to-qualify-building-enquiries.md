Most builders qualify on instinct. They talk to someone, form a view, and decide whether to price it.

Instinct is not bad. It is just inconsistent. It varies with how busy you are, what mood you are in, and how much you liked the person on the phone. The same enquiry that gets quoted in March gets ignored in November, and neither decision was made on evidence.

A qualification process is not a script that replaces judgement. It is the thing that makes sure judgement is applied to the same information every time.

## What you actually need to know

Four things decide whether an enquiry deserves a detailed estimate. Everything else is detail.

### 1. Budget, and whether it is real

Not "what are you hoping to spend". Hoping is aspiration, and aspiration is where the 30% to 60% gap lives between what homeowners expect and what a build costs.

Ask what their working budget range is. Then ask what that number is based on. The answers separate into three groups fast:

- A bank pre-approval or a completed finance conversation. Real.
- A figure from a display home or a per-square-metre number off a website. Usually 30% light and correctable, but you are now doing education, not quoting.
- Silence, or a number that moves when you push it. Not ready.

The middle group is not a dead lead. It is a lead that needs a conversation before it needs an estimate, and quoting them at this stage is what produces the quote that goes quiet.

### 2. Timeline, and what it depends on

"When are you hoping to start on site" is the question. The follow-up matters more: what has to happen first.

Land settled or not settled. Plans final or still moving. Council pathway understood or not started. Finance approved or in application.

A homeowner who wants to start in three months with unsettled land and draft plans is not starting in three months. That is not a problem, and it is a very good reason not to produce a fixed price today.

### 3. Whether the job is actually yours

Project type, complexity, and whether it sits in the zone where you make money.

Every builder has a band where they are genuinely good and genuinely profitable. Outside it, the job takes longer, the margin thins, and the client is harder to satisfy because you are learning on their house.

This is the qualifying criterion builders skip most often, usually when work is quiet. It is also the one that produces the jobs people regret.

### 4. Site and location

Within your working area, and with site conditions you can price without guessing.

A steep block, difficult access, or a soil classification nobody has checked is not a reason to decline. It is a reason to charge for the investigation before committing to a number.

## Asking without interrogating

The objection to structured qualification is that it feels like a form. Homeowners ring a builder because they want to talk to a builder, not fill in a survey.

Two things fix this.

**Explain why you are asking.** "I want to make sure I am giving you something useful rather than a number I have guessed" earns almost any question. Homeowners are not offended by rigour, they are offended by feeling processed.

**Ask the uncomfortable one early, not late.** Budget is the question builders defer, and deferring it is what costs the forty hours. Asked in the first conversation it is a normal professional question. Asked at hour thirty-five it sounds like the price is about to change.

## What to do with the answers

Qualification only pays if the answers change what happens next. Three outcomes.

**Ready and a fit.** Move to a site visit and, if you run one, a paid preliminary. These are the enquiries your process exists to find.

**A fit but not ready.** This is around half of them, and they are worth more than most builders think. Custom home cycles commonly run eight to twelve months from first contact. The correct action is a long, low-effort nurture, not a quote and not a deletion.

**Not a fit.** Say so quickly and warmly. A clear "this is not the right job for us, and here is who I would call" costs nothing and generates referrals for years.

## Why this matters more if you already charge

Builders who charge for preliminary work sometimes treat the fee as the qualification. It is not.

The fee filters people unwilling to spend money. It does not filter people whose budget is 40% short, whose land has not settled, or whose job sits outside your profitable band. Those people will happily pay a preliminary fee and then not proceed, and you will have converted an unpaid loss into a paid one with an unhappy client attached.

Qualification comes before the fee. The fee is what happens after somebody qualifies.

## Making it survive a busy week

The reason qualification fails is never that builders disagree with it. It is that at 6pm after a day on site, a returned call is a returned call and nobody is working through four questions.

Whatever you build has to hold when you are flat out. That usually means it does not live in your head. A structured first response that arrives immediately, the same questions asked the same way, and answers that are recorded rather than remembered.

That is the difference between having a process and intending to have one.

## Common questions

### Is it rude to ask a homeowner their budget on the first call?

No, provided you say why. Homeowners consistently report frustration at builders who avoided the budget conversation and then produced a number nowhere near what they could spend. Asking early is the respectful version.

### What if they refuse to give a budget?

Some genuinely do not know, which is a different answer from unwilling. If they do not know, give them ranges and let them react to a number rather than produce one. If they will not engage with cost at all, that is information.

### Should I quote someone who is not ready yet?

Usually not a detailed quote. A ballpark range costs you an hour and keeps the relationship alive. A detailed estimate for someone eight months from starting will be out of date before they decide, and you will have spent the hours twice.

### How many enquiries should I expect to disqualify?

Roughly two in ten are never going to build with anyone, and around half are a fit but not ready now. If you are quoting everything that comes in, most of that work is going nowhere by definition.
