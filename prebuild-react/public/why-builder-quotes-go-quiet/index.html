Quote conversion

Why quotes go quiet

The real reasons a homeowner stops replying after receiving a quote, why price is usually not one of them, and the follow-up pattern that recovers the ones worth recovering.

Updated 2026-07-25 · For Australian residential builders

You spent twenty hours on it. You sent it Thursday. It is now three weeks later and there has been nothing.

Most builders file that under "too expensive" and move on. Usually that is wrong, and it is an expensive thing to be wrong about, because the quotes that go quiet are the ones you already paid for.

Price is rarely the actual reason

When homeowners are asked afterwards why they did not proceed with a builder who quoted, price comes up, but it is seldom the deciding factor on its own. What comes up more often:

  • They were not as far along as they said, and the quote arrived before they were ready to decide.
  • Their budget was never close, and they discovered that from your number rather than knowing it up front.
  • Something in the process made them uncertain, and going quiet was easier than saying so.
  • Another builder simply followed up and you did not.

Only the second of those is really about money, and it is about their money, not your price. A homeowner carrying a 30% to 60% gap between what they expect and what a build actually costs is not rejecting your quote. They are absorbing news.

The uncomfortable version: if you found that out at hour thirty-five instead of hour one, that is a qualification failure, not a pricing failure.

The three buckets

Every builder's enquiry pipeline splits roughly the same way.

Around two or three in ten are a good fit and ready now. These are the ones you win, and they are the ones you would probably have won regardless.

Around five in ten are a good fit but not ready. Finance is not settled, the land is not settled, the plans are not final, the relationship has not decided. These are not bad leads. They are early. Custom home cycles frequently run eight to twelve months from first contact.

Around two in ten are never going to build with anyone. Price checkers, insurance jobs, people benchmarking a renovation they will not do.

The money is in the middle bucket, and the middle bucket is precisely the group that goes quiet. They are not rejecting you. They are not ready, and they have no reason to send an email saying so.

Why one follow-up is not follow-up

Most builders make one attempt after sending a quote, sometimes two, then stop. It feels like the polite ceiling.

The consistent finding across sales research in long-cycle purchases is that the attempt that converts is often the fifth or sixth. The gap between two attempts and six is not a small improvement, it is most of the recoverable value.

The reason builders stop is not laziness. It is that attempt three onwards feels like chasing, and no builder wants to look like they need the work. That instinct is sound and the conclusion is wrong. What makes follow-up feel like chasing is the content, not the count.

"Just following up on that quote" is chasing. It asks the homeowner to do something for you.

A follow-up that carries something useful is not. A note about the soil report timing on their block, a change in a material lead time, a similar build you have just finished, a plain question about whether their finance has settled. Each of those gives them a reason to reply that is not "have you decided about us yet".

What actually recovers them

A defined sequence, not a memory. If follow-up depends on the builder remembering while running three sites, it will not happen. It has to exist outside anybody's head.

Long horizons. If the custom home cycle is eight to twelve months, a follow-up sequence that ends at day thirty is measuring the wrong period. The quotes that reactivate often do so months later.

Value in each touch. Two to three useful contacts a month sustains attention. Pushing for a decision early in the relationship loses a substantial share of the list permanently.

Speed at the front. Responding within five minutes of an enquiry makes a builder several times more likely to qualify that lead than responding within an hour. Most of the reason quotes go quiet at the end is that the relationship started weakly at the beginning.

If you charge for preliminary work

This problem does not disappear when you charge. It changes shape and gets more expensive.

A homeowner who paid for a preliminary agreement and then went silent is a much bigger loss than a free quote that went nowhere, because they had already demonstrated they were serious. They cleared the filter. Something after the filter lost them.

Usually it is the same thing: nobody followed up in a structured way after the document was delivered. The paid preliminary was treated as the end of a process rather than the middle of one.

The honest arithmetic

Take your own numbers. Quotes sent in a year, minus the ones that signed. That is the pool.

Assume conservatively that only one in seven of those was genuinely recoverable with better follow-up. On a $650,000 average contract at 8% net margin, each recovered build is worth around $52,000 of margin.

You do not need many. That is the point that gets missed when follow-up is treated as admin rather than revenue.

Common questions

How many times should I follow up on a quote?

More than you currently do. Most builders stop at one or two, and the attempt that converts in long-cycle purchases is frequently the fifth or sixth. The constraint should be whether you have something useful to say, not a fixed count.

Is it worth following up on quotes from last year?

Often yes, particularly in a slow patch. Custom home decisions get deferred for finance, land and family reasons that resolve on their own timeline. A plain, unpushy check-in on old quotes is one of the cheapest sources of work available to a builder.

How do I follow up without sounding desperate?

Change the content, not the frequency. A message that carries information they do not have reads as professional. A message asking whether they have decided reads as chasing. The number of contacts is rarely what creates the impression.

Should I ask why they did not proceed?

Yes, and ask it plainly and without defending the quote. The answers are the most useful market data you will get, and they frequently reveal that the loss happened much earlier in the process than the quote.
